# Your Questions for Fortreum: Enterprise Account Executive

> **Prepared for:** Saul Cabrera | **Role:** Enterprise AE | **Date:** February 2026

Use this as your question playbook. You will NOT ask all of these -- pick 5-7 based on who you are talking to and what stage of the interview you are in. The stage selection guide at the bottom tells you which questions to pull for each conversation.

---

## Strategic Questions

### 1. "With the Gryphon investment closing in January, how is the go-to-market strategy evolving? Are you primarily focused on scaling existing account penetration or expanding into new logo acquisition?"

**Why this is a strong question:** It signals you have done your homework on the PE transaction and are thinking about how the investment thesis translates to the sales motion. It also helps you understand what the actual job looks like day to day -- hunter vs. farmer vs. hybrid.

**What to listen for:** Whether the growth strategy is land-and-expand within the existing blue-chip base (AWS, Palo Alto, zScaler) or aggressive new logo acquisition. If they say both, press for which has higher quota weighting. Also listen for whether Gryphon is bringing operational playbooks or letting the founders drive GTM.

---

### 2. "XRAMP seems like a fundamental shift from point-in-time to continuous assessment. How mature is the market's understanding of that value prop? Are you finding that most enterprise conversations require education first, or are buyers already asking for continuous assurance?"

**Why this is a strong question:** Shows you understand the core product thesis and are thinking about the real-world selling environment. It also demonstrates that you know the difference between selling a known category and creating a new one -- two very different motions.

**What to listen for:** If the answer is "we still need to educate," that means longer sales cycles and heavier discovery. If buyers are already asking for it, that means faster adoption but likely more competitive pressure. Either answer tells you how to calibrate your ramp plan.

---

### 3. "The CMMC C3PAO authorization opens a massive addressable market with 300,000+ DIB contractors. How are you thinking about segmenting that opportunity between enterprise DIB primes versus the mid-market supplier base?"

**Why this is a strong question:** CMMC is the biggest growth vector in Fortreum's near-term story. Asking how they segment it shows you understand that a Lockheed Martin engagement looks completely different from a 200-person subcontractor. It also signals you think about territory planning and ICP definition, not just "give me leads."

**What to listen for:** Whether they have a clear segmentation strategy or are still figuring it out. If it is still forming, that is an opportunity to position yourself as someone who can help build it. Also listen for whether CMMC is a separate team or folded into the enterprise AE book.

---

### 4. "What does quota look like for this role -- structure, ramp timeline, and what percentage of the team is hitting plan right now?"

**Why this is a strong question:** This is the single most important question for understanding whether the comp plan is achievable. Asking about team attainment percentage tells you whether the quotas are realistic or aspirational. Every serious sales candidate asks this -- not asking it signals you are not thinking like an operator.

**What to listen for:** If fewer than 50% of reps are hitting quota, that is a red flag about territory design, product-market fit, or unrealistic targets. If they dodge the question, note that. Also listen for ramp structure -- is it a full quota from day one, or is there a protected ramp period? Given the technical complexity of FedRAMP and CMMC, a 90-day ramp with reduced quota is reasonable to expect.

---

### 5. "Gryphon mentioned bringing AI and automation capabilities to enhance XRAMP. What does the near-term product roadmap look like for AI-powered features, and how should the sales team be positioning that today?"

**Why this is a strong question:** The Gryphon press release specifically calls out AI as part of the investment thesis. Asking about the roadmap shows you read beyond the homepage and are thinking about how to sell what is coming, not just what exists. It also positions your AI selling experience at Aisera and Agiloft as directly relevant.

**What to listen for:** Whether AI features are shipping soon or are still conceptual. If they are near-term, that gives you a differentiation story against Coalfire and Schellman. If they are further out, you know not to oversell capabilities that do not exist yet.

---

## Operator Questions

### 6. "What does the average win rate look like for the enterprise team, and what is the typical competitive loss reason?"

**Why this is a strong question:** This is a question a sales leader asks, not a sales candidate. It shows you think about the business at a team level, not just your individual deals. It also gives you critical intel on what objections you will face and whether the competitive positioning is working.

**What to listen for:** Win rate benchmarks (30-40% is healthy for enterprise, below 20% is a problem). The loss reasons matter even more -- losing on price means different things than losing on brand recognition or feature gaps. If they are losing to Coalfire on reputation, that tells you the XRAMP differentiation story is not landing yet. If they are losing on price to smaller 3PAOs, that tells you the value narrative needs strengthening.

---

### 7. "What is the current average deal size and sales cycle length for enterprise engagements? Is the expectation to move upmarket or maintain the current range?"

**Why this is a strong question:** Directly relevant to whether your experience profile fits. Your sweet spot has been $150K-$500K ACV at Agiloft. If Fortreum's deals are $200K-$2M, you are in range. If they are pushing into $5M+ territory, you need to understand the support structure around that. Asking about the direction also shows you are thinking about your own growth trajectory.

**What to listen for:** Match the answer against your own deal history. If the numbers align with your Agiloft and Aisera experience, say so in real time: "That maps well to what I ran at Agiloft." Also listen for whether deal size is growing because of XRAMP bundling -- that is a good sign for the platform thesis.

---

### 8. "Who are you losing deals to most often, and what is the competitive play when you do?"

**Why this is a strong question:** Shows competitive awareness and that you are already thinking about how to win. It is also a test of how transparent the hiring team is -- a good sales org can articulate their losses honestly. A weak one will say "we do not really have competition."

**What to listen for:** Expect to hear Coalfire, Schellman, or A-LIGN. The more interesting answer is the competitive response -- do they have battle cards? Competitive positioning docs? Or is each AE figuring it out on their own? If the latter, that is another area where you can add immediate value.

---

## Operational Questions

### 9. "What does the current account assignment model look like for this Enterprise AE role? Are these named accounts with existing relationships, or is there greenfield territory to develop?"

**Why this is a strong question:** A named account book with existing relationships means faster ramp and near-term expansion opportunities. Greenfield means longer time to revenue but bigger upside. Understanding the mix tells you how to plan your first 90 days.

**What to listen for:** Whether you are inheriting warm accounts (ideal for quick wins) or building from scratch. Also listen for account overlap with other AEs or internal teams. Territory conflict kills momentum.

---

### 10. "What does ramp look like for AEs in the first 90 days? Given the technical depth of FedRAMP and CMMC, how do you bring new AEs up to speed on the regulatory frameworks?"

**Why this is a strong question:** The compliance and regulatory knowledge required for this role is significantly deeper than typical SaaS. Asking about ramp shows you respect the domain complexity and are thinking realistically about how to become productive. It also surfaces whether they have structured onboarding or expect you to figure it out.

**What to listen for:** A structured ramp plan is a green flag. If the answer is "you will learn on the job," that is fine for a startup, but you should factor that into your ramp expectations. Also listen for whether there is a technical enablement resource (SE, Solutions Architect) who supports AEs during the learning phase.

---

### 11. "How is the relationship structured between the AE and the Delivery Lead? The JD mentions collaborating to identify whitespace -- what does that handoff and feedback loop look like in practice?"

**Why this is a strong question:** The AE-Delivery Lead dynamic is central to the expansion motion this role is built around. If delivery teams surface upsell intelligence back to the AE, that is a powerful engine. If there is a wall between sales and delivery, expansion opportunities get missed.

**What to listen for:** Whether delivery leads actively flag expansion opportunities or whether the AE has to dig for them. The best answer is a structured cadence -- weekly syncs, shared dashboards, or engagement health scorecards that surface whitespace. If it is informal, that is a process you can help build.

---

### 12. "What is the current tech stack for the sales org? CRM, forecasting, enablement?"

**Why this is a strong question:** A practical question that shows you care about the tools and infrastructure you will be working with. It also tells you how mature the sales operation is. A company with Salesforce, Clari, Gong, and a content library is operating differently than one running deals on spreadsheets.

**What to listen for:** You are proficient in Salesforce, Clari, Gong, Outreach, and ZoomInfo. If they use any of those, call it out immediately. If they are still building the tech stack, position your experience as an asset: "I have set up pipeline tracking and forecasting workflows in Clari before -- happy to help stand that up."

## Market-Aware Questions

### 13. "FedRAMP is pushing toward RFC-0012 and more aggressive vulnerability detection and response standards. How is that shift in the regulatory landscape creating opportunities or challenges for Fortreum's assessment approach?"

**Why this is a strong question:** This is the most technically informed question on this list. It signals you have done research beyond the company website and into the actual regulatory environment. Most candidates will not know what RFC-0012 is.

**What to listen for:** Whether they see regulatory changes as tailwinds (more assessment work, more complexity = more services sold) or headwinds (more stringent requirements = harder to deliver). The right answer is "both, and here is how we are positioning." Also listen for whether XRAMP is being updated to address these new requirements.

---

### 14. "With the founders retaining significant equity post-Gryphon, what is the long-term vision? Is the play to build the definitive platform in this space, or are there acquisition targets that could accelerate the portfolio?"

**Why this is a strong question:** Shows you think about the business at a strategic level and are evaluating Fortreum as a long-term career move, not just a job. It also surfaces the PE playbook -- Gryphon's typical hold period, whether they are building to sell or building to scale.

**What to listen for:** Listen for ambition. "We want to be the Salesforce of cybersecurity compliance" is a different answer than "We are focused on profitable growth." Both are valid but signal very different trajectories. Also note whether they mention M&A -- PE firms often use acquisitions to accelerate platform buildouts.

---

### 15. "What does success look like for the person in this role twelve months from now? What would make you say 'that was the right hire'?"

**Why this is a strong question:** Forces the interviewer to articulate their actual expectations beyond the JD. The answer gives you a cheat sheet for how to position yourself in every subsequent conversation.

**What to listen for:** Whether they define success purely by quota number or also by account health, relationship depth, and pipeline quality. A mature org will mention NRR, expansion pipeline, and customer satisfaction. A quota-only answer is not wrong, but it tells you the culture is heavily metrics-driven.

---

### 16. "The company values mention autonomy and accountability. What does that actually look like for an AE day to day? Is this a 'here is your territory, go execute' environment, or is there more structure?"

**Why this is a strong question:** Company values on a website are aspirational. How they translate to daily work is what matters. This question respectfully tests whether the stated culture matches reality, and it helps you understand the management style.

**What to listen for:** You want to hear something that matches your working style. If you thrive with autonomy (and your track record at fully remote roles suggests you do), listen for confirmation that AEs own their strategy. Also listen for how much CRM hygiene, pipeline review cadence, and reporting is expected -- that is the accountability side.

---

## Questions to AVOID

- Do not ask about base salary, OTE split, or benefits in the first interview (too early)
- Do not ask basic questions about what FedRAMP is (shows you did not do your homework)
- Do not ask "What does your company do?" -- you should already know
- Do not ask about PTO or work-life balance framing (signals wrong priorities for a growth-stage company)
- Do not ask how many AEs they have (this can sound like you are sizing up competition rather than the opportunity)

## DANGER QUESTION PREP

These are questions the interviewer might ask where you need a pre-rehearsed, honest, and confident answer. Do not wing these.

---

### "What is the largest deal you have personally closed?"

**Your answer:** "$500K ACV at Agiloft. It was a multi-stakeholder enterprise CLM deal with a 6-month sales cycle involving Legal, IT, Security, and Procurement. I built the business case, multi-threaded across four executive sponsors, and negotiated the enterprise licensing agreement."

**Coaching notes:** Do not inflate this number. $500K is a strong answer for the experience level and maps well to Fortreum's deal range. If pressed on whether you have closed $1M+ deals, be honest: "Not as the sole AE on record, but I have been involved in portfolio-level engagements at Jamf that exceeded $1M in aggregate ARR. I am ready to step into that range -- my deal mechanics and stakeholder management are built for it."

---

### "Do you have experience selling to federal agencies directly?"

**Your answer:** "I have not sold directly to federal agencies, but I have sold to companies that sell into government. At Rapid7, many of my accounts were companies with significant federal exposure, and at Agiloft, compliance and security reviews were a standard part of every enterprise deal cycle. What I bring is fluency in the compliance-heavy sales motion -- navigating security reviews, aligning to regulatory requirements, and building business cases around risk mitigation. The buyers I have sold to care about the same things federal buyers care about: security posture, audit readiness, and risk reduction."

**Coaching notes:** Do not try to stretch your experience into something it is not. The honest answer followed by a credible bridge is stronger than a vague claim. Fortreum's clients are CSPs and DIB contractors, not the agencies themselves -- so your experience selling to regulated enterprise buyers IS relevant. Make that connection explicitly.

---

### "Why did you leave Rapid7?"

**Your answer:** "I wanted to move into enterprise named accounts and longer, more strategic sales cycles. Rapid7 was a great learning experience -- it gave me my cybersecurity foundation and MEDDPICC training. But the mid-market transactional motion was not where I wanted to build my career. I wanted to run complex, multi-stakeholder deals with six-figure-plus ACVs, which is exactly what I did at Jamf and then Agiloft. Rapid7 was the right role at the right time, and it set me up for everything that followed."

**Coaching notes:** The short tenure at Rapid7 (6 months) will come up. Frame it as intentional career progression, not as a failure or a bad fit. Never speak negatively about Rapid7 itself. The key phrase is "I wanted enterprise named accounts" -- that directly connects to why you want the Fortreum role now.

---

### "The JD says 10+ years of experience -- you have 8. Thoughts?"

**Your answer:** "You are right, and I want to address that directly. I have 8 years of progressive enterprise sales experience. What I would point to is the intensity and complexity of that experience. In those 8 years, I have sold cybersecurity, AI platforms, device management, and contract lifecycle management -- all to enterprise buyers in highly regulated environments. I have consistently performed in the top 10% of my teams, with attainment ranging from 103% to 180%. I have run full MEDDPICC cycles against entrenched competitors, navigated InfoSec and compliance reviews as a standard part of my deals, and maintained zero churn while growing accounts through disciplined expansion. The question is not whether I have enough years -- it is whether I have the right experience and the right results. And I believe the track record speaks for itself."

**Coaching notes:** Do not be defensive. Do not apologize. Do NOT claim a hard NRR number -- describe the behaviors (zero churn, account growth). The framing is: quality and intensity of experience over raw tenure. The 110-180% attainment range is your strongest proof point. Deliver this with confidence, not with a "please give me a chance" tone. You are making a case, not asking for a favor.
