## Q3: "How do you approach building and executing an Enterprise Account Plan?"

**Answer (ready to deliver):**

My account planning starts with the customer's business trajectory, not their current contract. At Agiloft, before building any plan, I researched each account's regulatory landscape, upcoming contract renewals, M&A activity, and strategic initiatives.

The framework I use has four parts. First, stakeholder mapping. I identify every decision-maker and influencer, their priorities, and their internal politics. For a Fortreum account -- say a major CSP -- that means mapping the CISO, VP of Compliance, Cloud Engineering leads, and the federal sales team who needs the FedRAMP authorization to close government deals.

Second, whitespace analysis. What services are we providing today versus what the account could benefit from? If we are doing their FedRAMP assessment, are they managing CMMC, SOC 2, or PCI independently with another firm? That is a consolidation opportunity.

Third, a timeline tied to their business events. Compliance has natural triggers -- authorization renewal dates, contract bid deadlines, CMMC enforcement milestones. I align outreach to those moments so I am showing up with relevance, not just a calendar reminder.

Fourth, a mutual action plan the customer co-owns. When the customer has skin in the timeline, deal velocity increases and surprises decrease. At Agiloft, this approach helped me build $2M-plus in qualified pipeline and compress deal cycles by 20%.

## Q5: "How would you articulate the value of continuous assessment (XRAMP) versus traditional point-in-time audits to a skeptical CISO?"

**Answer (ready to deliver):**

I would not lead with the product. I would lead with their pain.

Something like: "You are managing six to eleven compliance frameworks with different timelines, different evidence requirements, and the same internal team stretched across all of them. Every year, your team goes through a massive scramble to prepare for assessments, then catches their breath, then does it again. How much of your compliance team's time is spent collecting the same artifacts for different auditors?"

That question usually opens the conversation. Every CISO I have spoken to at companies with complex compliance obligations knows the pain of the annual cycle.

Then I would frame XRAMP not as replacing what they do but as evolving it. "What if you distributed assessment activity across the entire year, aligned your frameworks so evidence submitted once satisfies FedRAMP, SOC 2, and ISO simultaneously, and had a predictable schedule your team could actually plan around?"

The business case goes beyond efficiency. Point-in-time assessments are snapshots. Between audits, controls drift, configurations change, and you are flying blind. Continuous assessment means continuous assurance. You catch issues in real time rather than discovering them twelve months later.

I would close with the board-level angle: "Imagine telling your board, 'Here is our compliance posture as of today' -- not as of your last audit nine months ago." That is the kind of confidence that changes the conversation.

The pattern is always the same: consume, shadow, teach back, iterate. I would apply the exact same approach to FedRAMP, CMMC, and XRAMP at Fortreum.

## Q21: "You have only sold SaaS products. How would you adjust to selling professional services?"
